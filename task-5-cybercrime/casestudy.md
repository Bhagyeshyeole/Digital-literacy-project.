Cybercrime Awareness Case Study:

So, let’s talk about that whole “urgent internship” email thing.

We all know phishing has been around forever. It’s not some new, high-tech thing. 
But honestly? That doesn’t make it any less dangerous. But that’s not really
how it works. It’s way more personal than that. It’s about messing with your head.
Think of it like digital camouflage. Someone builds a fake website that looks exactly
like the one you actually trust—your university portal, your student email login,
that kind of thing. And then they just… wait. 

They let you walk right in and hand
them your login info yourself. No breaking and entering. You just open the door.
 It’s the middle of the semester. You’re running on maybe three hours of sleep,
 you’ve had way too much coffee, and that background anxiety about not having 
 a summer internship yet is just constantly buzzing in your head.
 
Then, an email pops up.
It looks… totally fine. University logo at the top. Clean formatting. The font 
feels familiar. It’s signed off with something like “Placement Cell” at the bottom. 
Then you see the subject line: “URGENT: Complete your profile for guaranteed summer
internship selection.”

And just like that, you’re hooked.
The hook. There’s something about the words “guaranteed internship” that just hits
different when you’re stressed. Your brain doesn’t stop to ask questions. 
You click before you’ve even finished reading the sentence.

The mirror. The link takes you to what looks exactly like your Microsoft student
login page—or your university’s portal. Same colors, same boxes, same logos.
So you type in your email and password without thinking twice.

The ghosting. Then… nothing. You hit login and it just hangs. Sometimes it’s a
generic error message. Sometimes the little loading circle just keeps spinning.
You roll your eyes, mutter something about campus Wi-Fi being terrible again,
and go back to whatever you were doing.
Why this messes you up more than you’d expect

Once someone gets into your school email account, they’ll turn right around and use it to 
send that same fake link to everyone you know—your classmates, your professors,
your lab partners, maybe even your academic advisor. Suddenly, you’re the one 
spreading the scam without even knowing it. And cleaning that up? It’s a mess.
You’re stuck apologizing, explaining, resetting everything. And honestly, 
he hit to your reputation—digital or otherwise—takes way longer to fix than 
just changing your password.
