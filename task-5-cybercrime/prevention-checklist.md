Part B – Prevention Checklist:

A Few Things Worth Keeping in Mind
1. Your UPI PIN is for sending money. Not receiving.
Seriously, just tattoo this somewhere in your brain: you never, ever need to enter your UPI PIN to accept money.
If someone sends you a QR code and says, “Scan this to get your refund” or “just pay ₹1 to verify and you’ll 
get your cashback,” that’s a scam. Plain and simple. Scanning a code and punching in your PIN does exactly
one thing—it moves money out of your account. Receiving money? All someone needs is your UPI ID. No PIN, no drama.

2. Take two seconds to read the name before you tap pay.
We’re all in a rush—scanning QR codes at the chai stall, splitting a bill in the canteen, paying for a quick 
grocery run. But before you hit confirm, just glance at the name that pops up on your screen. There’s a scam
going around where people paste fake QR stickers over real ones in busy spots. The sticker looks legit, but
the money ends up going straight to them. That one quick glance before paying? It’s genuinely all it takes.

3. Lock your banking apps, not just your phone.
Your phone’s screen lock is great. But if you hand your phone to someone to make a call or show them a photo,
they shouldn’t be able to casually swipe over to your bank app or scroll through your WhatsApp. App locks 
take two minutes to set up, and they’re one of those things you’ll be really glad about if you ever need them.

4. Actually look at the link before you click.
I get it—we’re all guilty of just tapping links without thinking. But next time you get an email about 
“mandatory course registration” or some internship that sounds way too perfect, pause for a second. 
On a laptop, hover your mouse over the link and check the URL that shows up at the bottom of the screen. 
On your phone, hold the link down for a moment. If the address looks like keyboard smash or doesn’t 
end with your university’s official domain, close it. Don’t click, don’t “just check.”

5. Turn on two-factor authentication. Like, now.
Two-factor authentication is one of those things people put off, but honestly? It’s one of the simplest
ways to protect yourself. Even if someone manages to get your password, they can’t get into your account
without that one-time code that only your phone gets. Set it up on your email, your university portal, 
your social media—anywhere that offers it. Future you will be genuinely thankful.

6. No real internship asks you to pay first.
Let’s be clear: no legit company—especially not a well-known one—will ask you to pay a “security deposit
” or “laptop processing fee” before you even start. If anyone asks for money upfront for an internship,
it’s not an opportunity. It’s a trap. Walk away.

7. Switch to mobile data for anything financial.
Campus Wi-Fi and café hotspots are convenient, sure. But they’re also pretty easy for someone with a 
little know-how to snoop on. If you’re checking your bank balance or making a payment, just switch 
to your mobile data for that minute. It’s not about being paranoid—it’s just a small habit that keeps things safer.

8. Blur details before you post.
We all love sharing stuff—ID cards, hall tickets, internship offer letters. But before you put that
on Instagram or WhatsApp, take a second to blur out your roll number, address, date of birth, 
anything personal. Those details might seem small, but scammers use exactly that kind of info to 
make their fake emails sound eerily convincing. A quick blur takes a second and saves a lot of headache.

If Something Does Go Wrong
Look, if you realize you’ve been scammed, it feels horrible. But panicking just eats up the time that 
actually matters. Here’s what to do, step by step:

First, head to the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal and file a complaint. Do it as soon as you can.
Second—and this one’s more urgent—call 1930. That’s the national helpline for financial fraud. 
The sooner you report it, the better the chances that authorities can freeze the transaction before the money gets moved out.
